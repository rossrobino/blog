## Welcome to existence

Humans are born into the world and our senses are immediately flooded with inputs. We use our senses to learn, slowly and then faster when we can start communicating and reading. Each input we take in impacts our next decision. For example, baby reactions are very predictable: _hunger_ (cry), _rocking_ (rest), or _sees mom_ (excitement). As we grow, this gets more abstract. Maybe the input of our parents determines how we behave in school, or your wife prefers you to shave your beard.

Our lives are a series of inputs and reactions based on those inputs. While in many ways we are predictable creatures, there is a large variation in how people behave to the same inputs.

- People who I grew up with in very similar conditions have vastly different lifestyles than my family and me
- While I love the show _I Think You Should Leave_, many do not
- Even the same food can [taste different to two different people because their genetics are different](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1698869/)

It's common to predict people's actions and outcomes based on their traits and preferences. For example, personality type, IQ, or culture differences. These predictions are possible because people with similar inputs and circumstances often make similar decisions. They are more likely to act the same way as their peers in the same situation.

## Neural networks

Modern large language models (LLMs) such as GPT-5.4 or Claude Opus 4.6 are built out of neural networks. While being digital, conceptually neural networks aren't so different from the ones in our brains, hence the name.

> These networks consist of layers of interconnected neurons that process information:
>
> 1. An input layer where data enters the system. Input is converted into a numerical representation of words or tokens...
> 2. Many hidden layers that create an understanding of patterns in the system. Neurons inside the layer apply weights to the input data and pass the result through an activation function. This function outputs a value, often between 0 and 1, representing the neuron’s level of activation.
> 3. An output layer which produces the final result, such as predicting the next word in a sentence. The outputs at this stage are often referred to as logits, which are raw scores that get transformed into probabilities.
>
> ---[Understanding AI](https://leerob.com/ai#neural-networks) by Lee Robinson

AI goes through a training process that determines it's base reactions.

## Animal hierarchy of worth

Depending on your beliefs you might value different species over others. For me, and many others the hierarchy is something like this:

1. Humans
2. Pets - dogs, cats, horses
3. Smart animals - octopuses, dolphins, elephants
4. Other mammals
5. Fish
6. Reptiles
7. ...
8. Prefer dead - insects, mosquitos, spiders

Unless you value ants as much as humans, you have some version of this for yourself. Many pescatarians value fish lives less than other animals for example.

Where does AI fit into this? The main difference is biomatter. Does AI deserve more respect than an ant? Would you take a bite of it if it was prepared well?

## Religion provides direction

Without religion, everyone is quickly reduced to a mass of cells that happen to have joined together. What makes human thought from a brain any more valuable than what's going on in a GPU? Arguably, as LLMs become more advanced, they will solve problems a single human would not be capable of.

Christianity for example puts humans above all else. _People are created in God's image_ is an extremely pragmatic revelation. This quickly justifies having an animal hierarchy, and putting human life above AI.
