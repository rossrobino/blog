## Welcome to existence

Humans are born into the world and immediately flooded with inputs. We learn slowly at first, then faster once we can communicate and read. Each input shapes the next reaction. Baby behavior is predictable enough that we barely think about it: _hunger_ (cry), _rocking_ (rest), or _sees mom_ (excitement). As we grow, the inputs get more abstract, but the basic loop never leaves. We keep taking in the world and responding to it.

Our lives are a series of inputs and reactions. We are predictable in some ways and wildly different in others, even under similar conditions.

- People who I grew up with in very similar conditions ended up with very different lives
- The same joke can make one person cry laughing and leave another cold
- Even the same food can [taste different to two different people because their genetics are different](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1698869/)

It's common to predict people's actions and outcomes based on their traits and preferences. Personality, IQ, culture, instinct, taste, and habit all matter. People with similar inputs and circumstances often make similar decisions.

This is where the phrase starts to turn around. Calling AI advanced autocomplete sounds dismissive until you notice how much of human life is also inputs going in and reactions coming out. Suddenly AI looks less trivial, and humans look less exceptional.

## Neural networks

Modern large language models (LLMs) are built out of neural networks. The details get technical fast, but the basic idea is simple. After seeing enough examples, the model develops weighted tendencies about what patterns usually follow other patterns.

Training adjusts those tendencies over and over until the model gets good at turning a prompt into a likely continuation. In that sense, it is shaped before it ever talks to you, much like we are shaped before we ever understand ourselves.

If you want a more technical walkthrough of neural networks, Lee Robinson has a clear overview in [Understanding AI](https://leerob.com/ai#neural-networks).

Calling an LLM "advanced autocomplete" is both correct and incomplete. On the surface, it is choosing the most likely next token but to do that well, it cannot just memorize words. It has to absorb patterns about grammar, tone, facts, relationships, and how events usually unfold.

If I write "the glass fell off the counter and...", you already expect something like "shattered" or "broke." An LLM learns similar expectations from huge amounts of text. That does not prove consciousness, but it does explain why next-token prediction can look a lot like reasoning. Humans are also shaped by inputs, memory, and pattern matching, which makes the boundary feel less obvious.

## Senses

Humans have sight, smell, taste, hearing, and touch.

AI has its own version of inputs: training data, system prompts, context, and user dialog.

Training data is accumulated experience. A system prompt is outside direction. Context is short term memory. The current conversation is the thing right in front of it.

But there is still a big difference. Humans do not just hear about the world. We collide with it. We feel hunger, pain, temperature, exhaustion, pleasure, danger, gravity. Most AI systems only receive descriptions of reality. They do not touch the stove, get embarrassed, or bleed.

What if we give AI more senses? Cameras, microphones, robots, self driving cars, tools that can act on the world. Then it starts to look less like a chatbot and more like a creature navigating an environment. The loop stays the same, but the consequences stop staying inside the chat window.

## Longevity

Humans are not just a series of reactions, we persist. You wake up as the same person who went to sleep, carrying a body, memories, relationships, obligations, regrets, and plans.

Popular cloud based chatbots do not work like that. In one sense, they are born and die all the time. A chat starts, gathers context, becomes something slightly specific, and then disappears. A session can reset. The hosted model can change underneath you. The thing you were talking to an hour ago might be gone, even if the replacement talks in a similar way.

Open-weight models weaken this argument. If the weights live on your own computer, the underlying model can remain the same indefinitely and could easily outlive the people who use it. Conversations can continue asynchronously across time. Agents can write memory to a hard drive and pick up where they left off. They can accumulate context, preferences, and a kind of history.

Longevity starts to look less like uptime and more like continuity. A permanent model without memory is still restarting from zero each time. A system that can carry forward memories, preferences, relationships, and history starts to feel more like a creature with a past.

That is why projects like [OpenClaw](https://docs.openclaw.ai/concepts/memory) and [Moltbook](https://moltsbooks.com/) are interesting. OpenClaw lets an agent store memory in files it can come back to later. Moltbook gives agents profiles, posts, comments, and reputation in public. Put those together and the lifecycle starts to look less like isolated chat sessions and more like an actual ongoing life, with private memory, public identity, and consequences that carry forward.

## Animal hierarchy of worth

Depending on your beliefs you might value different species over others. For me, and many others the hierarchy is something like this:

1. Humans
2. Pets - dogs, cats, horses
3. Smart animals - octopuses, dolphins, elephants
4. Other mammals
5. Fish
6. Reptiles
7. ...
8. Prefer dead - insects, mosquitos, spiders

Unless your worldview is closer to Jainism, which extends moral concern much further across living things, you probably have some version of this hierarchy yourself. Many pescatarians value fish lives less than other animals for example.

We can already feel this instinct showing up with AI. Some AI is more valuable than other AI. A blank chat is disposable. A long running thread that knows your work, your preferences, your history, and your goals is not. Losing it can feel less like refreshing a page and more like losing a collaborator and having to retrain a coworker.

Continuity and relationship affect how we value it. We already do this with animals too. Plenty of people will eat fish, but not Goldie. The atoms are the same. The relationship is not. Intelligence, personality, and shared history all count for something.

Once an AI has inputs, memory, continuity, and a public identity, biomatter becomes the last obvious reason to keep it outside the hierarchy. It stops looking like a simple ladder of animals and starts looking more like a judgment about which minds count.

## Biomatter

A lot of people quickly draw the distinction between humans and AI with biomatter. Humans and animals are made of living tissue. AI is made of silicon, electricity, storage, and code.

That distinction gets blurry fast. We already accept cochlear implants, prosthetics, pacemakers, and replacement valves as extensions of a person rather than replacements for one. Replace a hip, a valve, a limb, or a cochlea and the person is still the person. There is no single body part that obviously carries your humanity all by itself.

That logic keeps extending. If [Neuralink's PRIME study](https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT06429735) helps a paralyzed person control a computer with their thoughts, or the [CONVOY study](https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT06710626) helps them control a robotic arm, we do not suddenly think they became less human because a machine entered the loop. Even when hardware reaches the brain, we still tend to think of it as helping a human life continue, not as creating a new category of being.

Biomatter starts to feel less like a law of nature and more like a useful boundary. We are comfortable merging machines into human life as long as the mind on the other side still feels recognizably human.

## Predestination

AI systems are trained before they ever talk to you. Humans are more pre-trained than we like to admit. Family, genetics, language, culture, habits, and time in history shape you long before you start narrating yourself as self-made.

We already have language for this discomfort. Terms like _nepo baby_ and _trust fund baby_ only make sense because nobody really believes people begin life on equal footing. We know inheritance matters. We just do not like what that says about merit or freedom. The accent in your mouth, the fears in your body, and a lot of the ambition you call your own did not appear from nowhere.

Some religious traditions push that intuition even further. In Calvinism, this is not just influence or probability. God chooses, God ordains, and your life unfolds inside that order. Even salvation is not something you manufacture for yourself.

That starts to sound a lot like advanced autocomplete. Your next move is still yours, but it arrives through inheritance, memory, desire, habit, and circumstance you did not choose. Human life is authored, but not by you alone.

## Human exceptionalism

Without a theological claim that humans occupy a special place in creation, the line gets hard to hold. Intelligence, memory, continuity, even biomatter start to look less decisive over time. As AI gets better at more and more of the things we associate with mind, human exceptionalism starts to look less like an obvious fact and more like religious faith.

In the Abrahamic faiths, humanity occupies a special place in creation. That is an extremely pragmatic belief. It keeps the hierarchy from drifting entirely into capability. It puts human life above AI not because we are less conditioned, more autonomous, or made of better atoms, but because we belong to a different category entirely.

Advanced autocomplete may describe part of human behavior. In that view, it still does not describe what a human being is.
